# ADHD Drug Access Research Repository

The purpose of this repository is to gather together some notes and sources about a topic I feel passionate about: the control of ADHD medication. This notebook is open-source by design—anybody can read it on GitHub.

## Some Foundational Context

At the time I write this (November 7th, 2025), I am 36 years old. I live in Israel. Several years ago, I was diagnosed with ADHD.

ADHD patients encounter a lot of skepticism and prejudice. I believe it to be entirely unfounded. At this point in my life, I also don't feel the need to argue with or convince anybody that ADHD is real—nor that it's a neurological phenomenon.

ADHD medication has been a life-changing experience for me.

I have replaced a lifelong habit of self-medicating ADHD with caffeine and non-effective supplements (ugh) with something that works reliably well: Vyvanse (Lisdexamfetamine), which, thanks to government subsidies, costs me less than I previously spent on coffee beans and other paraphernalia (I miss Turkish coffee, but only a little).

It has its downsides, sure: it has taken me a year of working with my doctor to find a balance. Hyperfocus is real. But much as SSRIs often afford depressed patients the mental quietude to explore therapy while in a point of stability, the stability of an effective stimulant has afforded me the focus to explore non-drug avenues like time management.

The opportunity cost of *not* treating ADHD is also, I believe, far more profound than just letting "scattered people be less focused" (I characterize social tropes here without endorsement). The costs of lost productivity are not just personal—they are societal. Societies flourish when individuals contribute to the fullest extent their capacities allow. So long as ADHD medications can demonstrate efficacy and safety, I believe there is a compelling case to be made in favor of ease of access for patients.

## The Bureaucratic Nightmare

Several years ago, I stumbled upon an article in The Guardian which I found humorous only because it matched my own experience so perfectly. I include it in the repository as additional context.

ADHD medications are controlled substances. But the full extent of the bureaucracy that patients face in accessing these medications is often so overwhelming that I have, at times, just given up on accessing them.

To provide some examples:

- During my titration onto the medication, I moved from 30mg to 50mg. I presented my doctor's prescription. I was informed that I would have to bring my half-used medication to the pharmacy in order to receive the new dose. No problem! I did so. But then watched in disbelief as the pharmacist emptied and then meticulously counted the returned medicine pill by pill in front of waiting customers. Then, dismay—I was one short! As a matter of course, I like to keep one "backup" pill of each of the three daily meds I take in my glove compartment lest I be out of the house at dosing time. I had to drive home to find the one missing pill, return to the pharmacy, and explain the situation. Only then could I receive the 50mg tablets.

- I have at various times been told that I can refill my script every 30 days, 29 days, 28 days, or 27 days. The feeling is that the pharmacist just makes it up.

- The policy in Israel as implemented currently (at least as I have experienced it) means that patients are permitted precisely one tablet per day of their medication. I have seen the problems that this creates at various levels. For one, it means that patients have zero margin for error: if you, say, lose one tablet (when I used the water titration method, I threw out the dose by accident more than once), you are out of medication. During the Iran-Israel war in Israel, pharmacies were closed—and the Jewish calendar sometimes means that your refill date is not fillable. The Iranian war in particular meant that I had to go cold turkey for two days while attempting to keep abreast of fast-changing news and find safe shelter!

- The procedure for getting advance medication before traveling abroad is unwieldy. The local pharmacist has to refer the form up to the regional pharmacist who has to individually approve every application. Before a recent trip to the US, the approval did not come through in time. I was told that this was a bureaucratic error. My trip was more stressful because I departed knowing that I did not have sufficient medication.

In all these instances, the "system" has behaved as if not supplying sufficient medication were no big deal and akin to maybe asking somebody to skip coffee.

## Reasonable Policy

If I had time for such an initiative, down the line, I would love to rally together adults with ADHD to push for awareness and change for what I see as a significant problem: obtaining dependable access to legally prescribed ADHD medications, whether they be controlled or not.

Hawking's article highlights that the problems in the current approaches are actually multifaceted: artificial quotas capping supply and creating needless drug shortages; insurance companies "passing on the pain" of coverage to patients; and finally, pharmacies enforcing often unreasonably onerous policies.

The effects, I believe, are far-reaching: ADHD patients often have to drag themselves through complex diagnostic processes to receive a diagnosis but are then left fighting for treatment.

As Hawking's article points out, there is a rich irony in forcing ADHD patients—for whom executive dysfunction may be a challenge—to navigate such complexities.

Finally, different global legal treatment poses another challenge: the ADHD subreddit contains some lively discussions about the status of ADHD meds in Japan. Japan requires paperwork, but reports of bureaucrats being unfamiliar with the policies operated abound. All this creates more guesswork, complexity, and stress for patients simply trying to stay on an effective medication.

We have seen, in recent years, moves to improve access to other medications subject to legal restriction—namely medical marijuana. Unfortunately, stimulants seem to have been skipped over in this push.

## What Would "Reasonable Access" Look Like

Here is what reasonable continuity of treatment would *feel* and look like to me:

- I know that I can get my meds when prescribed and that I won't face weird hurdles in doing so. I have been taking an asthma inhaler for years and the process comes with zero complexity: I can present a script and get 3 months supply at a time. I have never faced a single obstacle or question when doing so!

- I know that I am legally allowed to hold a "backup" of up to (say) X pills. The factoring for allowable refills has to afford patients some leeway for emergencies and the vagaries of calendars. Forcing ADHD patients to have exactly one medication per day is asking them to walk a tightrope for no good reason and adds needless stress to our lives. Patients could perhaps have an annual emergency supply which they can fill once per year in addition to their regular refills.

- The procedure for advance medication for travel should be straightforward and handled in an efficient and fully digital manner. There should be no surprises or miscommunications between the pharmacy and health fund.

- Education: pharmacists and some doctors need to understand that just because ADHD stimulants don't have dangerous withdrawals, "cold turkey" events due to supply disruptions can still have extremely serious ramifications including impaired care to dependents, missed work, etc. SSRI withdrawals are also not life-threatening, but the medical system is evolving to treat them with understanding. Stimulants should be treated similarly.

I believe that there also needs to be more understanding of the fact that patients with ADHD and those illicitly using stimulants are very different. I heard one friend affirm that Vyvanse is basically "legalized speed". A simple comparison between medical and illicit doses does a lot to dislodge beliefs like this.

## Illicit Supply

The prescribed nature of ADHD medications exists because of their potential for addiction—and for illicit secondary markets that result secondary to poor prescribing practices.

My contention is that while these are real and valid problems, the vast majority of ADHD patients who take their medications as prescribed should not pay the hefty penalty.

The illegal production of stimulants is a policy for drug enforcement. Illicit supply and overprescribing may perhaps be best tackled at their source: physicians who may be prescribing these drugs without necessary oversight.

It is an unfortunate reality that legally prescribed stimulants and those used illicitly share a molecular structure—such that they are one and the same under drug codes.

A suggestion of my advocacy movement would be that this is unfair and that medically prescribed stimulants be treated separately to avoid the potentially devastating consequences arising from innocent mistakes.
